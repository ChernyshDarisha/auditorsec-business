# Phase 1: Core Team Assembly & Commitment - Strategic Planning Guide

## **Executive Summary**
Phase 1 establishes the foundation for project success by assembling a cross-functional core team of 5-7 key stakeholders who possess decision-making authority, relevant expertise, and genuine commitment to the initiative. The objective is securing 100% participation through structured recruitment, formal commitment protocols, and clear accountability frameworks within 7 business days.

---

## **1. Team Composition Strategy**

### **Core Team Structure (5-7 Members)**

#### **Decision Authority Layer (2-3 members)**
- **Executive Sponsor**: C-level leader with budget authority and strategic oversight
- **Project Champion**: Senior director with operational authority and resource allocation
- **Subject Matter Expert**: Technical/functional lead with deep domain expertise

#### **Implementation Layer (3-4 members)**
- **Operations Lead**: Process owner with implementation capability
- **Communications Lead**: Messaging and stakeholder management expertise
- **Analytics Lead**: Data analysis and performance measurement capability
- **Customer/Market Lead**: External perspective and validation authority

### **Composition Rationale**
- **Size**: 5-7 members ensures diverse perspectives while maintaining decision efficiency
- **Authority**: Each member must have direct influence over their functional area
- **Expertise**: Combines strategic vision with operational execution capability
- **Representation**: Covers all critical stakeholder groups affected by the initiative

---

## **2. Recruitment & Selection Process**

### **Step 1: Stakeholder Mapping (Day 1)**
- Identify all departments/functions impacted by the project
- List potential candidates with relevant authority and expertise
- Assess availability, workload, and commitment capacity
- **Criteria**: Decision authority (High), Domain expertise (High), Time availability (Medium)

### **Step 2: Direct Recruitment (Days 2-3)**
- **Executive Sponsor recruits personally** - demonstrates priority and commitment
- **Individual 15-minute conversations** - explain importance, role, expectations
- **Clear value proposition** - what's in it for them and their department
- **Timeline transparency** - specific time commitments and deliverables

### **Step 3: Commitment Securing (Days 4-5)**
- **Formal commitment documentation** - signed agreements with specific obligations
- **Calendar coordination** - block time for key activities (workshops, reviews)
- **Resource allocation confirmation** - ensure adequate bandwidth for participation
- **Backup identification** - secondary options if primary candidates unavailable

### **Selection Criteria Matrix**
| Criteria | Weight | Evaluation Method |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Decision Authority | 30% | Can they approve changes in their area? |
| Domain Expertise | 25% | Do they understand the technical/functional aspects? |
| Stakeholder Influence | 20% | Can they drive adoption in their department? |
| Time Availability | 15% | Can they commit required hours? |
| Communication Skills | 10% | Can they articulate positions and facilitate discussions? |

## **3. Commitment Framework**

### **Commitment Levels**
- **Level 1 - Champion**: Actively advocates, dedicates maximum time, owns outcomes
- **Level 2 - Supporter**: Participates fully, contributes expertise, implements decisions
- **Level 3 - Contributor**: Attends sessions, provides input, follows through on assignments

### **Accountability Structures**

#### **Formal Agreements**
- **Time Commitment**: Specific hours per week/month with blocked calendar time
- **Deliverable Ownership**: Clear responsibility for specific outcomes and deadlines  
- **Decision Authority**: Documented scope of approval/veto power
- **Communication Obligations**: Regular status updates and stakeholder management

#### **Engagement Protocols**
- **Weekly check-ins**: 15-minute progress calls with project lead
- **Monthly reviews**: Formal progress assessment with executive sponsor
- **Quarterly adjustments**: Team composition and commitment level reassessment
- **Success sharing**: Recognition and reward for exceptional contribution

### **Long-term Engagement Strategies**
- **Career development tie-in**: Link project success to individual performance goals
- **Organizational recognition**: Public acknowledgment of contributions and achievements
- **Resource support**: Provide necessary tools, training, and administrative assistance
- **Feedback loops**: Regular input on process improvements and obstacle removal

- [ ] **Signed commitment agreements** from all team members
- [ ] **Calendar coordination complete** for next 30 days of activities
- [ ] **Stakeholder communication** completed to relevant departments

### **Engagement Quality Metrics (Weeks 2-4)**
- [ ] **Meeting attendance**: 95%+ participation in scheduled sessions
- [ ] **Deliverable completion**: 100% on-time delivery of assigned work
- [ ] **Communication consistency**: Weekly updates provided as scheduled
- [ ] **Decision-making efficiency**: Consensus reached within agreed timeframes

### **Sustainability Indicators (Month 1)**
- [ ] **Commitment maintenance**: No dropouts or significant engagement decline
- [ ] **Proactive participation**: Team members initiating ideas and solutions
- [ ] **Stakeholder advocacy**: Team members promoting project in their departments
- [ ] **Resource acquisition**: Teams securing necessary resources for their contributions

## **Risk Mitigation Strategies**

### **High-Risk Scenarios**
| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| Key stakeholder unavailability | Medium | High | Identify 2-3 alternatives per role |
| Commitment level degradation | Medium | High | Regular check-ins and engagement reinforcement |
| Authority/decision-making conflicts | Low | High | Clear role definitions and escalation protocols |
| Resource constraints | High | Medium | Executive sponsor commitment to resource provision |

### **Early Warning Indicators**
- Delayed responses to recruitment outreach
- Requests to reduce time commitments before starting
- Multiple scheduling conflicts for initial meetings
- Pushback on commitment documentation requirements
